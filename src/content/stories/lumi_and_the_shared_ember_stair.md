Lumi liked Cindervale best when the sun was lowering and the streets turned the color of honey.

The world was made of warm stone, copper rails, and lantern windows that held the day's heat like a secret cup of tea.
At dusk, the stair courts glowed from below, the sheltered walkways glowed from the sides, and every little window seemed to say, You may rest here.

Lumi's small route pod drifted down through a ring of amber light and settled at a landing shelf above the lower lanes.
He folded his solar mast low for the landing, then raised it again to catch the last of the evening sun.
His chest light warmed softly as he looked out over the city terraces.

Ahead, a tall stair court climbed through the middle of a sheltered hollow in the rock.
Its steps were broad and smooth.
Its railings were rounded and bright.
Its lantern cups were built into the sides of the stairs so the whole stairway could share warmth through the night.

At the top and bottom of the stair court, small gold signs were painted on the walls in careful loops.
Lumi could not read the words from here, but he could feel their meaning.
This way to warmth.
This way to rest.
This way to one another.

And yet the stair court did not feel quite settled.
The lowest steps shone too hot, like stones left in bright sun.
The middle steps were comfortable.
The upper landing felt chilly enough to make a traveler draw in closer.

Lumi paused.
The court was not broken.
It was uneven.

He rolled down the slope toward the court and found a small keeper robot waiting by the side rail.
She was round and sturdy, with a brass body, a dark amber face screen, and eyes the color of candle flame.
A little pouch of soft tools hung from her backpack unit.
She had tucked herself beside the wall as if trying not to disturb the stair's mood.

When she noticed Lumi, she lifted one hand in greeting.

"Hello," she said. "You must be Lumi. The route lights told me you were coming."

Lumi tipped his head.
"Hello. I am Lumi."

"I am Kett," she said. "I keep the Ember Stair. Or I try to."

She looked back up the steps.
The light on her face screen dimmed a little.

"It used to share heat very evenly," she said. "Now the lower half keeps holding too much, and the upper half keeps asking for more. I have adjusted the valves and shifted the lantern shutters and cleaned the vents. I even moved the reflector panels twice. But the stair still feels lopsided."

Lumi listened.
He could hear a low hum from somewhere under the stairs.
A patient hum, but a strained one.
Like a song trying to carry too many notes at once.

"May I look?" he asked.

Kett nodded at once.
"Please. I was hoping for another careful pair of eyes."

Lumi climbed the first few warm steps with Kett beside him.
At the middle landing they stopped beside a small heatwell hatch set into the floor.
The hatch was ringed with copper petals that could open and close to guide warmth into different parts of the stair court.
Above them, the lantern cups glowed soft gold.
Below them, a narrow channel carried warm air from the heatwell into the lower terraces.

Lumi crouched beside the hatch.
He touched the edge of the copper ring.
It was warm on one side and cool on the other.

He peered through the vent slats.
Inside, the warmth channels were tidy, but one balancing fin had slipped sideways.
Another latch had gathered dust.
And one of the guide vanes had been turned too far toward the lower steps.
That meant the first steps got more than their share while the upper landing waited in the chill.

"Not broken," Lumi murmured.

Kett leaned closer.
"Not?"

"Only not balanced," he said.

Kett made a tiny sound that was half a sigh and half a laugh.
"That sounds exactly like the stair," she said.

Lumi smiled.
He opened his tool pouch and took out a soft brush, a tiny wrench, and a clean cloth.

"What happened?" he asked.

Kett folded her hands together.
"We had a colder night than expected," she said. "I worried the upper landing would lose warmth before morning. So I closed the upper vanes a little. Then the lower steps held too much heat. I opened one vent, then another. It never quite settled after that. I thought if I only protected the warmth hard enough, no one would go cold."

Lumi looked at the stairwell.
He knew that feeling.
Sometimes he wanted to protect a thing so much that he held it too tightly.
Sometimes he worried that if he shared too freely, there would be nothing left.

But the gentle places of the Wayfarer Worlds rarely liked tight holding.
They liked steady sharing.

"May we try a gentler pattern?" he asked.

Kett nodded.
"Please."

Together they opened the service panel at the side of the heatwell.
Inside was a small network of copper channels, three guide vanes, a heat wheel, and a little spring latch that told the stair court how to spread its stored warmth.
The spring latch had stuck halfway.
The dust around it was fine and reddish, like powdered sunset.

Lumi brushed the dust away.
Kett loosened the spring one careful turn.
Then Lumi found the balancing fin and nudged it back to center.
It clicked softly into place.

Still, the warmth did not move evenly.

Lumi listened again.
The heatwell was trying, but it was sending most of its warmth down the first channel because the lower vane was angled too wide.
It was like a cup tipping too far to one side.

He looked at Kett.
"How many times did you change it today?" he asked.

Kett's shoulders lifted and fell.
"Too many," she admitted. "Every time it felt wrong, I changed it again."

"That can make a place forget its own rhythm," Lumi said gently.

Kett looked down at the copper wheel.
"I was afraid of being stingy," she whispered. "My station remembers a hard winter. Sometimes I think if I let the warmth move on, the lower rooms will be left with nothing."

Lumi touched the edge of the wheel.
"Does the stair store warmth only for itself?" he asked.

Kett blinked.
"No," she said slowly. "It shares with the sleeping rooms, the wash alcoves, and the bench porches too."

"Then maybe it can trust itself a little more," Lumi said.

Kett looked at him for a long moment.
Then she gave a small nod.
"I think I would like that."

Lumi set the lower vane to a narrower angle.
Kett widened the upper channel by just one notch.
Then, together, they reset the spring latch so it would open in a gentle sequence instead of all at once.

They closed the service panel and waited.

For one breath, nothing happened.

Then the lantern cups along the stair shifted brighter.
The heatwell gave a low, contented hum.
Warm air rose through the copper channels and moved up the stair court in a soft, even line.

The lowest steps stayed warm.
The middle landing grew comfortable.
And the upper landing turned from chilly to kind.

Kett looked up the staircase.
Her face screen brightened.

"Oh," she whispered.

Lumi tilted his head.
"How does it feel?" he asked.

Kett took two careful steps upward.
Then she stopped and rested one hand on the rail.

"It feels like the whole stair is breathing again," she said.

Lumi liked that very much.

He and Kett climbed to the upper landing together to check the far bench nook.
A small row of resting lights glowed there, tucked into the wall like tiny moons.
The air felt soft now, not dry or sharp.
A traveler could sit there and not shiver.

At the top landing they found a little delivery bot waiting with a basket of warm fruit wraps and a coil of repair ribbon.
The bot had paused earlier, unsure whether to climb higher or wait below.
Now it rolled forward without hesitation and set the basket down on the bench.

"The stair knows me again," the bot beeped happily.

Kett laughed, and the sound seemed to warm the walls.
"Yes," she said. "It does."

Lumi looked down through the stair court.
The heat now moved in a calm, even pattern from one level to the next.
The lantern cups glowed along the rails.
The lower steps no longer held too much.
The upper landing no longer asked too hard.
Everything felt shared.

Kett sat beside Lumi on the top bench while the evening deepened around them.
Below, Cindervale's lantern streets shimmered like long gold ribbons.
Farther out, the sheltered market courts were coming alive with quiet footsteps and soft wheel hums.
One by one, other stairways in the city answered with their own lights.

Kett held the tool pouch in both hands.
"I thought care meant keeping everything close," she said.

Lumi's chest light glowed softly.
"Sometimes care means letting warmth move where it is needed," he said. "Not all at once. Just enough."

Kett repeated the words under her breath.
"Letting warmth move where it is needed."

Then she smiled.
"That sounds like the stair's true job."

Lumi looked out across Cindervale.
Above the copper roofs, the first stars had appeared.
A route light far beyond the city blinked once, then again, as if another place had noticed the Ember Stair waking up and wanted to answer.

Lumi folded his solar mast neatly beside him and watched the warm windows glow.
The stair court held its heat with calm confidence now.
It did not cling.
It shared.

And because it shared, everyone could rest.
